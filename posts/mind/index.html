Recent Thoughts on Consciousness

Since my starting an undergraduate degree earlier last year, I have been thinking more seriously about theory of mind, probably because I joined my universities EA branch, and they enjoy that kind of discussion. Obviously I've had thoughts about this kind of thing before, but being forced to concisely and, more importantly, consistently describe my fuzzy brain-renderings in societies like EA means that I suddenly had a need to precisely lay out my preferred models for consciousness, mind, and free will. With that introductory preamble out of the way, let's get into the meat of the topic!

Non-Falsifiable Ideas

I pretty much universally take the stance that 'if an idea is not falsifiable, then it is not worth discussing'. This might be in poor taste in purely philosophical contexts, but in 'practical' situations (like EA), where we care about updating our beliefs and actions in a rational way, it obviously makes absolutely no sense; if AAA is not falsifiable, then by definition AAA can have no impact on the universe, and so it is not important to discuss. Moreover, even if AAA had an impact on unobservable universes that we might care about (for some reason), then we would still not be able to rationally change our actions since we can have no evidence about AAA's truth value.

Free Will

As an immediate corollary of this assertion, free will does not exist (at least not in the generally agreed-upon sense). This is easy to see; 'free will' in the normal sense requires some sort of unpredictability, and then, by the free will theorem, so do some elementary particles, contradicting the general notion of free will (NB: FWT is kind of controversial, and may not generalize to stochastic world-models, but I suppress this discussion for another time, because I haven't thought about it enough yet).

Some have argued that the 'free will' Conway and Kochen discuss is unrelated to the 'free will' in everyday parlance, but I disagree with this compatibalist take. In my experience, when people exercise 'free will', they implicitly expect that their decision-making process is somehow unpredictable, and yet the compatibalist stance opposes this. I take Kant's view, that any attempt to redefine free will to make it vacuously true is pure anthropocentric copium.

Non-Falsifiable Ideas in EAs

The above stance also applies to theories of consciousness and moral realism. It surprises me how many EAs continue to espouse the identification of conscious entities or the search for an objective set of true morals, even after defining consciousness as something entirely indeterminable (e.g. 'capacity to perceive') or providing literally no definition for 'true' other than restating the word.

Unfortunately, trying to explain my issues with these kind of ideas is very often a glacial process. While I don't fully understand why this is the case, I feel like the issue is that we (quite naturally) identify strongly with ideas that are intrinsic to our identity but are experimentally untestable (like the ability to perceive, or the sense of moral justification), and are forced to invent fictions about their relevance so that we can usefully function.

Conclusion
