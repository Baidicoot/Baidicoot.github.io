Grounding Ethics in Unfalsifiable Ideas

Oftentimes I find myself confused when trying to reflect on how I ground out my ethical principles and beliefs, in the broader context of living as an approximate consequentialist. I am pretty partial to ethical frameworks which consider entities with qualia to be moral patients, but unfortunately we currently have no experimental method for determining if a given entity experiences qualia, and it seems plausible that we could be in a world where such a method could simply not exist (e.g. see David Chalmers, "The Conscious Mind", Chapter II). If we assume we are in such a world, we encounter a particularly egregious type of moral uncertainty where we cannot make scientific progress on a property which is central to our moral intuitions.

Here, I will try and explain my thoughts on why we should not reject qualia as a morally-relevant property, how we can try to make moral progress under various assumptions about how indeterminable qualia is, and whether and when this becomes practically relevant for humans to deal with.
