Polysemanticity with Variable Sparsity

In this post I analyze the 'ReLU output model' from Anthropic's excellent Toy Models of Superposition paper, with the goal of figuring out how varying the sparsity (roughly, how likely something is to be zero) of the features (inputs to the model) changes what features the network learns and how. I start with some initial analysis of how variable sparsity affects the loss function with the aim of determining what features will be prioritized, what features will be in superposition, and what features will simply not be represented in the network. People familiar with Anthropic's paper can probably skip much of the preamble. I assume knowledge of what superposition is.

Initial Exploration

I motivated my initial exploration by analyzing the case where ϕ\phiϕ is the identity function (i.e. the model is purely linear), the bias is zero (as was learned for represented features in Anthropic's paper) and I have that E[zi]=0\mathbb{E}[z_i] = 0E[zi​]=0 and Var[zi]=1\mathrm{Var}[z_i] = 1Var[zi​]=1. The expected loss can then be simplified to

E[L]=∑i,j≠iIiPj(Wi⋅Wj)2−2∑iIiPi∣Wi∣2(∣Wi∣2−2).\mathbb{E}[L] = \sum_{i,j \neq i} I_i P_j (W_i \cdot W_j)^2 - 2 \sum_i I_i P_i |W_i|^2(|W_i|^2 - 2).E[L]=i,j=i∑​Ii​Pj​(Wi​⋅Wj​)2−2i∑​Ii​Pi​∣Wi​∣2(∣Wi​∣2−2).

The first term in the above equation can be viewed as representing the losses due to interference, and the second term can be viewed as the losses due to under/overrepresenting feature iii in the representation space. Note that the network is incentivized to represent a given feature proportionally to its relative importance and the frequency of its occurrence.

In light of this, I decided to contrast the case where Ii=1I_i = 1Ii​=1 (i.e. uniform importance) and Pi=0.5×0.93iP_i = 0.5 \times 0.93^iPi​=0.5×0.93i to the case where Ii=0.5×0.93iI_i = 0.5 \times 0.93^iIi​=0.5×0.93i and Pi=0.0714P_i = 0.0714Pi​=0.0714 (the mean sparsity of the other case).

I trained 1024 models for each case with feature-space dimension 100 and representation-space dimension 40 for 10000 steps using Adam with batch-size 1024. We can explain this phenomenon via inspection of the loss function. The elementwise linear loss LiL_iLi​ contains the term:

∑j≠iPj(Wi⋅Wj)((Wi⋅Wj)−2Pi).\sum_{j \neq i} P_j (W_i \cdot W_j) ((W_i \cdot W_j) - 2P_i).j=i∑​Pj​(Wi​⋅Wj​)((Wi​⋅Wj​)−2Pi​).

Assume for a simplification that feature embeddings can only be perfectly aligned, perfectly orthogonal, or perfectly antipodal, that is, Wi⋅Wj∈{1,0,−1}W_i \cdot W_j \in \{ 1, 0, -1 \}Wi​⋅Wj​∈{1,0,−1}. Then, the above term is minimized when the largest features are stored in antipodean pairs with the smallest features, which is what we see in the actual trained models.
